Low Blood Pressure (Hypotension)

Overview

Low blood pressure, also called hypotension, happens when the blood flowing through your arteries pushes with less force than normal. This can make it harder for your body to deliver enough oxygen and nutrients to the brain, heart, and other vital organs.

While some people naturally have low blood pressure without problems, in others it may cause health concerns, especially if it drops suddenly or stays very low for a long time. Doctors usually check blood pressure using two numbers — systolic (when the heart beats) and diastolic (when the heart rests). A reading lower than 90/60 mmHg is generally considered low.

Prevention and Care

Drink plenty of water
Eat balanced meals (avoid skipping)
Stand up slowly from sitting/lying down
Wear compression stockings if advised
Follow doctor’s advice for medication or treatment

Symptoms

  • Blurred or fading vision
  • Dizzy or lightheaded feelings
  • Fainting
  • Fatigue
  • Trouble concentrating
  • Upset stomach

Causes

  • Dehydration
  • Blood loss
  • Certain medicines (for heart or depression)
  • Heart problems (weak heart, valve disease, arrhythmia)
  • Hormonal issues
  • Standing up too quickly (orthostatic hypotension)
